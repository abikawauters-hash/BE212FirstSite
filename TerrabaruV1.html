The Science

Turning waste into a carbon time capsule

Biochar is produced through pyrolysis — the controlled heating of organic material without oxygen. The result is a stable, carbon-rich material that sequesters carbon for hundreds to thousands of years, while simultaneously improving the health of soils, water systems, and communities.

TerraBaru engineers biochar to exact specifications, tuning its characteristics for targeted applications across agriculture, construction, and energy sectors.

01

Agricultural Waste Collection

Rice husks, sawdust, corncobs, coffee husks, palm EFB — sourced from local farming communities.

02

Pyrolysis & Engineering

Controlled heating without oxygen converts organic waste into stable biochar. Properties are deliberately tuned per application.

03

Carbon Permanence

The resulting carbon is locked in a stable form — permanently sequestered, measurably verified, and internationally certified.

04

Soil, Infrastructure & Energy

TerraKarbon products are applied across agriculture, construction materials, and renewable energy systems.

Our Product

TerraKarbon

A stable, carbon-rich material with wide-ranging benefits for agriculture, infrastructure, and the climate. Produced to international standards through the pyrolysis of agricultural waste.

What is TerraKarbon

Biochar, precisely engineered

Biochar is charcoal made through pyrolysis — heating organic material without oxygen, converting it into a stable form of carbon. Unlike conventional charcoal, TerraKarbon is engineered to exact specifications for its intended application.

The characteristics of each TerraKarbon product are deliberately tuned — particle size, surface area, pH, mineral content — to deliver targeted results across soil health, construction materials, water filtration, and energy applications.

"Biochar is essentially a time capsule for carbon — locking it in a form that persists for hundreds to thousands of years while simultaneously improving the systems it is applied to."

Carbon Removal

The carbon cycle we close

Unlike nature-based carbon offsets that carry reversal risk, biochar carbon removal is scientifically proven to be stable for centuries. Our process turns atmospheric CO₂ — already captured by crops — into permanent soil carbon.

01

Crops absorb CO₂

Rice, palm, coffee, and corn absorb atmospheric carbon as they grow, storing it in biomass and agricultural residues.

02

Waste becomes feedstock

Rather than burning or decomposing (releasing CO₂ back to air), agricultural waste is collected and converted via pyrolysis.

03

Carbon is locked permanently

The resulting biochar contains stable carbon that persists for 500–3,000+ years — measured, certified, and applied to soil or structure.

Business Model

How TerraBaru
creates value

A diversified, scalable business model built around the production and distribution of engineered biochar — with revenue generated across agricultural markets, carbon credit systems, and infrastructure applications.

Revenue Model

Three primary revenue streams

Primary

Agricultural Biochar Sales

Direct sales of TerraKarbon products to plantation operators, smallholder farmers, and agricultural distributors across Indonesia and export markets. Revenue scales with production volume.

High-growth

Carbon Removal Credits

Each tonne of biochar produced generates certified, permanent carbon dioxide removal credits tradeable on voluntary and compliance carbon markets globally. Pricing reflects permanence premium over nature-based offsets.

Emerging

Infrastructure Applications

Biochar-enhanced construction materials and water filtration systems represent a growing addressable market, particularly across infrastructure-intensive Southeast Asian economies.

Operational Structure

A network of production sites

TerraBaru's footprint follows its feedstock — establishing production facilities in proximity to large-scale agricultural waste streams across Indonesia, and scaling globally where comparable feedstock availability exists.

Feedstock Sourcing

  • Structured supply agreements with local farming communities
  • Seven distinct waste stream types: rice husk, palm, sawdust, coffee, cocoa, corn
